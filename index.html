Why three

The discipline is the strategy

Three isn't a tidy number chosen for convenience. It is the maximum that working memory can reliably hold — and the number that Steve Jobs enforced with deliberate ruthlessness.

Every year, Apple gathered its top 100 leaders for a strategy retreat. They debated around ten projects. Then Jobs made them cut the bottom seven. Only the top three got any time or any money. His test was subtraction: “I’m as proud of what we don’t do as I am of what we do.” The discipline wasn’t a constraint on ambition. It was what made the ambition real.

Three is not an arbitrary tidy number. Working-memory research is blunt: people reliably hold three to four things at once — and in organisations with five stated priorities, only one in four managers can correctly name three of them. A strategy nobody can recall is a strategy nobody can act on.

Crucially, at Apple those three priorities did not only focus the leadership team. They focused every individual in the organisation — the engineer, the designer, the person at the Genius Bar. Everyone could answer the same question: what are we trying to do? Nova works the same way. These three pillars focus the work of every single person — from the classroom to the boardroom. A teaching assistant, a site manager, a CEO: all three questions, every day.

The pillars are permanent. Annual priorities sit beneath them and change as the organisation improves. What stays constant is the question every person in Nova is expected to answer: how am I contributing to Teamwork, Innovation and Results?

Onboarding a school, launching a digital programme, building leadership capacity — these are priorities, not strategy. They sit beneath the pillars and serve them. Every priority has a single named owner so accountability is never ambiguous.

The pillars don’t change. The priorities beneath them do.

This is what gives the strategy its stability and its flexibility at the same time. Three questions you never stop asking. Different answers every year.
